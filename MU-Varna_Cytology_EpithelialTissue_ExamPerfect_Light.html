Chapter Summary: Epithelial Tissue (Exam‑Perfect, Light)

High‑yield, mechanism‑first, and trap‑aware notes aligned to typical MU‑Varna MCQ patterns (classification, junctions, basement membrane, secretory epithelia, and transport).
CYTOLOGY EPITHELIAL TISSUE MU‑VARNA‑MCQ
3 Core Ideas (must recall in 20 seconds)
  • Epithelia are polarized, avascular sheets that rest on a basement membrane and specialize in barrier, transport, and secretion.
  • Classification is morphological: by layers (simple vs stratified vs pseudostratified vs transitional) and by cell shape (squamous/cuboidal/columnar), using nuclei as the light‑microscopy clue.
  • Junctions + basal lamina maintain compartments: tight junctions seal and separate apical/basolateral domains, adherens/desmosomes provide mechanical strength, hemidesmosomes anchor to basal lamina, gap junctions communicate.

I. Defining Features of Epithelia

Polarity: apical surface faces a lumen/external environment, basolateral surface interfaces with neighboring cells and underlying connective tissue. Tight junctions help keep these domains distinct (functional “fence”).
Avascularity: nutrients and O2 diffuse from vessels in the underlying connective tissue across the basement membrane; capillaries do not enter epithelium. Nerve fibers may penetrate the basement membrane. fileciteturn6file16

Exam trap: “cell shape” is often inferred by nuclei

  • Squamous cells → flattened nuclei
  • Cuboidal/pyramidal cells → round/spherical nuclei
  • Columnar cells → elongated nuclei
In routine H&E, membranes are hard to see. Use nuclear shape + number of nuclear rows to classify an epithelium. fileciteturn6file1

II. Basement Membrane: Structure & Functions

“Basement membrane” (LM term) includes basal lamina + reticular lamina. Basal lamina is adjacent to epithelium; reticular lamina is from connective tissue. fileciteturn6file2

Basal lamina (epithelium‑derived)

  • Type IV collagen network (sheet‑like mesh)
  • Laminin binds integrins and links to the collagen network
  • Nidogen + perlecan cross‑link laminin ↔ type IV collagen, regulate porosity/filtering

Reticular lamina (connective‑tissue‑derived)

  • Type III collagen meshwork
  • Type VII collagen anchoring fibrils bind reticular lamina to basal lamina

Key functions (MCQ‑friendly)

  • Semipermeable filter for diffusion to epithelial cells (classic example: renal glomerulus). fileciteturn6file1
  • Structural support and attachment of epithelium to connective tissue.
  • Maintains polarity by organizing integrins and membrane domains; scaffold for rapid repair after injury. fileciteturn6file3

IV. Junctions: Components, Cytoskeleton, and Clinical Links

Junction Major proteins Cytoskeleton link Key function
Tight junction (zonula occludens) Claudins, occludin, ZO proteins — Seal paracellular path, maintain apical/basolateral domains fileciteturn6file6
Adherens junction (zonula adherens) E‑cadherin + catenins Actin filaments Mechanical linkage between neighboring cells, stabilizes tight junction region fileciteturn6file6
Desmosome (macula adherens) Desmoglein, desmocollin; plakoglobin; desmoplakin Intermediate filaments (keratin) Strong spot‑weld adhesion (tissue integrity under stress) fileciteturn6file9
Hemidesmosome Integrins (notably β4) Intermediate filaments Anchor cell to basal lamina fileciteturn6file10
Gap junction Connexins → connexons — Intercellular communication (ions, small molecules) fileciteturn6file4

Clinical / exam‑relevant associations

  • Claudin/occludin disruption → increased paracellular leak; tight junction proteins can be targeted by pathogens; claudin disruption contributes to fluid loss into lumen. fileciteturn6file6
  • Loss of E‑cadherin in carcinomas → facilitates invasion/metastatic shift. fileciteturn6file0
  • Pemphigus vulgaris: autoantibodies against desmogleins → loss of keratin‑linked adhesion → blistering. fileciteturn6file4
  • Epidermolysis bullosa: integrin‑β4 mutations (hemidesmosomes) → fragile dermal‑epidermal attachment → blistering. fileciteturn6file0

V. Apical Specializations: What They “Do”

Microvilli

  • Increase apical surface area for absorption (e.g., intestine).
  • Associated with the terminal web (actin‑based network at apical pole). fileciteturn6file9

Cilia (motile)

  • Move mucus/fluids along epithelial surface (respiratory tract, oviduct).
  • Clinical pattern: decreased ciliary function → recurrent infections (mucociliary clearance failure). (Concept reinforced in chapter self‑assessment.) fileciteturn6file13

Stereocilia

  • Long microvilli‑like projections (non‑motile): epididymis, inner ear sensory epithelia (function depends on location).
Trap: “Cilia” vs “microvilli.” Cilia are for movement; microvilli are for surface area.

VI. Secretory Epithelia & Glands

Definitions

  • Glands are organs (or cells) specialized for secretion.
  • Exocrine glands have ducts; endocrine glands have no ducts and release hormones to interstitial fluid/blood. fileciteturn6file8

Exocrine gland architecture

  • Simple = unbranched duct, Compound = branched ducts. fileciteturn6file8
  • Secretory portions: acinar/alveolar, tubular, or mixed (tubuloacinar). fileciteturn6file8

Modes of secretion (classic MCQ)

Mode Mechanism High‑yield example Exam trap
Merocrine Exocytosis of vesicles (most common) Many protein‑secreting glands; mammary proteins Default unless specified otherwise fileciteturn6file14
Holocrine Whole cell disintegrates, releases product + debris Sebaceous glands (acne context) “Cell becomes the secretion” fileciteturn6file14
Apocrine Apical cytoplasm pinches off with product Mammary lipid droplets Not “exosomes”; larger apical blebs fileciteturn6file14

Mucous vs serous (staining logic)

  • Mucous: glycosylated mucins, stain poorly with H&E, stain well with PAS; goblet cells are unicellular mucous glands. fileciteturn6file8turn6file14
  • Serous: enzyme/protein secretion, abundant RER + granules → stain darker with H&E (basophilia). fileciteturn6file8
  • Myoepithelial cells: actin‑positive contractile cells around acini/ducts aiding secretion expulsion. fileciteturn6file5
High‑yield stain trap: PAS highlights carbohydrate‑rich mucins/glycoproteins; serous cells look “darker” largely because of RER + granules.

VII. Transport Across Epithelia: Absorption vs Secretion

  • Absorption: lumen → interstitium/capillaries (apical → basolateral direction). Example: intestine, gallbladder. fileciteturn6file13
  • Secretion: capillaries → lumen (basolateral → apical). Example: glands, choroid plexus. fileciteturn6file13
  • Tight junctions are essential to keep apical and basolateral compartments separated in both processes. fileciteturn6file13
MCQ framing: “Tight junctions prevent paracellular movement” and also act as a “fence” restricting membrane protein diffusion between domains. fileciteturn6file6

VIII. Renewal, Metaplasia, and Exam‑Level Clinical Hooks

  • Epithelia typically renew rapidly; basal cells often function as progenitors (conceptual link to stem/progenitor hierarchy from prior chapter).
  • Chronic bronchitis (smokers): increased goblet cells → excess mucus, impaired clearance, airway obstruction; can trigger metaplasia of pseudostratified ciliated epithelium into stratified squamous. fileciteturn6file15
  • Junction defects produce barrier failure or blistering phenotypes (tight junction proteins, desmosomes, hemidesmosomes). fileciteturn6file4turn6file0

Trap checklist (quick)

  • “Transitional epithelium” is not pseudostratified and is found only in urinary tract; hallmark is umbrella cells. fileciteturn6file15
  • “Basement membrane” vs “basal lamina”: LM term vs ultrastructural layer. fileciteturn6file2
  • Desmosome = cell‑cell, hemidesmosome = cell‑ECM (basal lamina). fileciteturn6file10
  • Adherens = actin; desmosome/hemidesmosome = intermediate filaments (keratin). fileciteturn6file12turn6file7
